Multicolored Logical Net Modeling
Formalism

This page introduces the qualitative dynamical systems' graphical modeling formalism, referred to as "the Multicolored Logical Net", or the McLN. Created in the form of McLN, models are based on the notion of the bipartite directed graph, and, therefore, can be assembled on a computer screen. Elements of these models are representations of such elements of the modeled systems as the Properties, Conditions, triggering changes of state of the Properties, Dependencies of the state of Conditions on the state of Properties and Dependencies of the state of Properties on the state of Conditions. Therefore, the McLN models can be, naturally, created as the graphical nets that are characterized by their structure, and by the mechanism of reproducing behavior of the modeled systems that is enabled by endowing the elements of the structure with the ability to perform operations of the Algebra of Symbols.

McLN Model Structure

The McLN model is constructed of two types of nodes: Property nodes, depicted as the big circles or big hexagons, Conditions, depicted as small circles, and two types of Arcs directed form the Property nodes to the Conditions nodes, and from the Conditions nodes to the Property nodes. Simple example of the McLN model is presented in Figure 1.

Figure 1. One Property depending on three situations.

Property nodes represent properties and states of the properties of the modeled systems, where the states are denoted as solid color that fills the circles or the hexagons. Conditions nodes are the conditions of changes of states of Property nodes. They represent facts of existence of expected situations that cause Property nodes, dependent on occurrence of situations, to change their states. Hence, states of Conditions nodes may either represent the fact that the expected situation is recognized or the fact that it is not recognized. These states are represented as the solid white color with symbol "+" drawn on it, or as just the solid gray color, respectively.

Arcs directed from a group of the Property nodes to a Condition node represent dependence of the fact of recognition of an expected situation on a certain combination of states of Property nodes. These arcs are marked with the colored dots, particular colors of which are those expected states that the arcs’ input Properties should have in order for the situation to be deemed as existing and recognized.

Arcs directed from a Condition node to some Property nodes represent dependence of the state of the Property nodes on the fact of the existence of the expected situation. These arcs have a small arrow heads marked with that colors of the proposed states which the arcs' output Property nodes should take when the state of the Condition node indicates the fact that the expected situation exists.

So, the structure of the McLN literally means that the state of each Condition node depends on the state of one or more of its input Property nodes, characterizing a particular expected situation determined by the colors of the dots marking the Properties' output arcs.

At the same time, the state of each Property node depends on the state of one or more of its input Condition nodes, and takes state represented by the color of the arrowhead of the arc directed form the Condition node to the dependent Property node in case when the state of the Condition indicates that all its input Properties represent expected situation denoted by the marks of their output arcs.

Exclusion>: Case when the state of Condition indicates that the expected situation is not recognized, or case when two or more Conditions suggest that dependent on different recognized situations Property should take different states are ignored and current state of the dependent property stays unchanged.

The Mechanism of the McLN model's Behavior

Unlike the mechanism for computing the XDNF expression, based on the idea of traversing the expression calculation tree obtained as a result of grammatical analysis of the expression, or the mechanism for computing the matrix equation of the qualitative system state, performed as first multiplying the System State Vector by the Situations Recognition Matrix, then multiplying the Facts Vector by the Generation Matrix producing the Proposed Values Vector, and finally applying the Application operation to it to get the System Next State Vector, the process of transformation of the current state of the McLN model into its next state is controlled by the flow of events.

For that purpose the Simulation Engine, that provides the work of the McLN model has and maintains the Event Queue. Whereas, the model behavior, also provided by the Simulation Engine, consists of two parts: "Model Initialization" and "Model Behavior Controller".

Part: Model Initialization

Part "Initialization" is executed only once. It first, identifies all situations present in the model existing as the result of assigning to Property nodes their initial state defined during creation of the model; and then places events in the Event Queue to inform the Simulation Engine that some of the expected situations exist and, thus, the process of the model's responses to the situations can begin.

Part: Model Behavior Controller

Part: Model Behavior Controller has several tasks to perform. It checks whether the Event Queue contains any events and if the queue is not empty - responds to these events, during which transforms the current state of Properties into their next states doing this for those properties changes of which require by the events which have occurred. Then it checks if just made changes of the state of the properties formed new situations and if yes checks if just arisen situations have dependent on them Properties. And if they have and the states of these Properties have to be changed, Controller creates for them Property State Change Events and places them in the Event Queue. This package of events will be processed at the next iteration of the Controller, during which the transformation of the states of the Properties, referred to in the events, will be carried out and the newly updated state of the model into its next state will be performed, and so on.

Thus, the main content of the Model Behavior Controller is a process consisting of: changing the state of the Model Properties, recognizing the situations that have emerged as a result of these changes, determining the changes in the properties corresponding to the aroused situations and placing, in the Event Queue, of the Property Change Events informing the Controller about the need to change state of the properties that must be processed at the next iteration of unloading the event queue.

Mechanism of Transformations of State of the McLN Model

Although pure mathematically, the bipartite directed graph is merely two sets of nodes connected by the directed arcs, the McLN model of a qualitative dynamical system, structure of which is based on the bipartite graph, in addition to the roles assigned to the elements of the graph, described above, should also be capable of reproducing behavior of modeled system.

And that means the graph should have in its possession a collection of functions associated with its elements. These functions have to ensure certain transformations of states of Property nodes, and the motion of their states from some Property nodes to others. And, thus, all that activity should be performed by the Property nodes, Condition nodes and by the both types of the arcs of the graph itself. And this is achieved by endowing each element of the model with the ability to perform the AoS operation, associated with it.

When describing transformation of state of McLN Model to the next state performed by the part: Model Behavior Controller, it is best to consider: 1) elements of a model as some kind of simple discrete devices, 2) work of the model (i.e., its behavior) as the interaction of these devices, and 3) the mechanism for controlling the work of the model as the process of performing functions associated with these devices.

Execution of the model assumes that the states of its all properties are always defined. Accordingly, performance of the operations of the elements of the model always begins with identifying situations present in the model.

Arcs directed from Properties to Conditions are comparators. Each arc implements the Equivalence operation, which compares the color of its input Property with the color that marks the arc.

Conditions perform recognition of the fact of presence of the expected situation in the model. Therefore, each Condition implements the Conjunction operation, whose task is to compute the conjunction of all the results of comparisons performed by its input arcs.

Arcs directed from Condition to Properties are generators of the proposed state of their output Properties. They implement Production operation. The task of each such arc is to produce a proposed state represented by the color marking the arrowhead of the arc in the case when the state of its input Condition is the fact of the existence of the expected situation. Or, alternatively, to produce a proposed state represented by the color indicating that the expected situation is not recognized.

Properties accept results produced by arcs directed to them from all Conditions. Each Property accepts state computed based on all proposed states produced by all generator arcs entering it. And since the new state of Property may be not always computed, the Property implements two operations - Disjunction and Application. Disjunction operation computes final proposed value, and Application operation either changes the color of the property to the color computed by the Disjunction operation, when it is defined, or leaves the state of the Property unchanged when the result of the Disjunction operation turns out to be undefined.

Mechanism of Interactions of the McLM Model With Outside World

Like any other dynamic system, a qualitative dynamical system can interact with the world around it, thet is to experience the influence of the world and influence it.

Analysis of changes in state of Properties of modeled qualitative systems allows us to notice two main reasons of causing their changes. These are changes caused by external processes that are observed as spontaneous changes in the state of some properties of the system that, like all other changes, participate in the formation of the situation. And changes in the state of Properties caused by internal processes of the model, the reasons for which are previously occurring changes in the state of Properties caused either by the dependence of these Properties on the expected situation, i.e., as a result of the process of simulating a change in the state of the model on the situation, or spontaneous changes caused by external influences. In both cases, changes in state caused by the dependence of the state of a Property on its own state that arose earlier, as changes caused by external influences, are perceived as spontaneous.

Reproduction of spontaneous changes in the state of properties, the cause of which is not known and therefore not described in the Qualitative Systems Behavior Specification, is implemented in the McLN models by including in the description of those Properties that may act spontaneously, lists of rules called either the Time Driven Program and the State Driven Program.

Properties that have a program associated with them are depicted on the model graph in the form of hexagons, where the number of ticks that must pass before it changes its current state to the state described in the program is depicted using a number placed inside the hexagons. This number is the countdown to the moment when the change of the state of the property caused by the program to occur.

For example, all the properties located in the bottom row of Figure 1 are the ones that have a Time Driven Program, and that, therefore, they are depicted in the form of hexagons. And that the numbers shown inside these hexagons indicate that there are only two ticks left before the pre-programmed change of the state of these properties will take place.

Example of Qualitative System Model Based
on McLN Modeling Formalism

Presented below example is the McLN model of popular discrete dynamical system widely known as the "Dining Philosophers". States of properties of this system are considered qualitative and the mechanism of its behavior is based on the Qualitative Systems Behavior Specification described on the page: "Qualitative Dynamical Systems, Their Behavior, and Mathematical Modeling" in the section: Qualitative System Behavior Specification . The McLN view of this model is presented in the figure 2.

Figure 2. The McLN Model of qualitative dynamical system:
“Dining Philosophers”.

This, more comprehensive than the presented in Figure 1, McLN model consists of five property nodes, representing Philosophers that are depicted on the outer radius of the model as the big hexagons, and five property nodes, representing Chinese Chopstick, that are depicted on its inner radius as the big circles. It serves for reproducing behavior of five asynchronous processes causing fragmentary changes in the state of both types of the system's properties.

Structure

According to specified behavior of the modeled system, Philosophers can take four successively occurring states: “Strolling and Contemplating” (blue), “Feeling Hungry” (red), “Dining” (magenta) and “Satisfied” (light green). Chopsticks may be in only two states - “Available” (green) or “Busy” (yellow).

Transitions of Philosophers from state "Strolling and Contemplating" to state "Hungry", and from state "Dining" to state "Satisfied" happen for unknown reasons, which, therefore, are deemed as unexplained factors. All that is known about these transitions is only that both of these changes occur spontaneously, but always require that some small random time has to pass after the Philosophers acquire states causing these changes. These transitions are handled by the Simulation Engine as external events generated by the State Driven Program associated with each Philosopher node, which are depicted in Figure 2 as hexagons.

Condition for philosophers to transition from state "Hungry" to state "Dining" is the situation that occurs when the philosophers are in the "Hungry" state and both Chopsticks located to the left and the right of them are in the "Available" state. Occurrence of this situation transfers such philosophers to state "Dining" and both Chopsticks to the "Busy" state.

Condition for Philosophers to transition from state "Satisfied" to state "Strolling and Contemplating" is the situation that occurs when the Philosophers have finished dining and are in the "Satisfied" state. Occurrence of this situation transfers Philosophers from the state: "Satisfied" to the "Strolling and Contemplating" state and both Chopsticks to the "Available" state.

Behavior

In the initial state of the model, all Philosophers are walking in the garden and are, thus, in the "Strolling and Contemplating" state. Therefore, simulation of behavior of dining philosophers starts as their transitions from the "Strolling and Contemplating" state and is performed as both driven by the external and internal events,

Transition from the state: "Strolling and Contemplating” to the state: "Feeling Hungry" occurs for a reason not presented in the model and is therefore perceived as spontaneous. It is implemented as an external event generated by the State Driven Program that has a requirement to replace state: "Strolling and Contemplating" with the state: "Feeling Hungry" after the random number of ticks.

Transition from the state of "Feeling Hungry" to the state “Dining” depends on the availability of both Chopsticks and is therefore determined by the moment of occurrence of the expected situation arising when both Chopsticks necessary for the beginning of the meal are in state “Available”. Simultaneously available Chopsticks allow Philosophers to start dining immediately. However, facing a case when either one or both Chopsticks are in “Busy” state, a particular Philosopher has to wait until one or both of his neighbors at the table to finish dining and release the Chopsticks they are using.

Duration of dinner, as well as the duration of the walking in the garden, also depends on many factors unknown to the model; and is, therefore, also implemented as the operation of the State Driven Program, which replaces state: “Dining” with the state: “Satisfied”, in a random number of ticks after the dining has begun.

While in the “Satisfied” state, Philosophers release both Chopsticks to the table and, thus, make them available to the other Philosophers who may be at the table to the left or to the right of them, and move to the state: "Strolling and Contemplating”, starting, thus, a new iteration of the strolling and dining.

Both examples, presented in Figure 1 and Figure 2 are developed in the "Qualitative Dynamical System Modeling & Simulation Environment" - the software application developed for creating McLN models and simulation of their behavior.

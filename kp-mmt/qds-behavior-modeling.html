Qualitative Dynamical Systems Behavior
Mathematical Modeling Technique
(Kaleidoscope Project)

While studying behavior of the dynamical systems phenomenologically (that is not by delving into their internal machinery, but instead considering only the abstract collections of properties of the systems, changing over time) we, following the fundamental principles of mathematical modeling technique, tend to explain causes of changes of properties of the systems by existence (in the systems) of certain dependence relations of state of the properties on the changes of the state of some other properties of the systems.

Correspondingly, the content of qualitative dynamical systems mathematical modeling technique is described further on as a complex of coordinated modeling solutions defining the way of creation of models of qualitative dynamical systems by describing them as collections of qualitative variables, representing qualitative properties of modeled systems, on the one hand, and describing dependence relations of states of the properties on the situations arising in the systems by representing this dependence, in the models, in the form of mathematical qualitative functions computing dependence of their values (and, thus, the states of the variables) on the states of other variables (characterizing situations) that are given as the arguments of the functions, on the other hand. Solutions to these challenges are described below.

General Form of Models of Qualitative Dynamical Systems

Perhaps the first most question related to the model defining solutions should be "How the model intended for simulation of the behavior of a qualitative dynamical system is supposed to be defined" and the answer to this question should sound as one of the solutions:

The model should be created as a collection of variables representing properties characterizing behavior of the existing or designed modeled qualitative system. These variables should be capable of representing qualitative states of the modeled properties as symbols taken from some set of symbols, for instance, the set of letters of some alphabet.

Then the behavior of the model being created for reproducing behavior of the qualitative dynamical system that is observed as the sequence of changes of the state of properties of the system, caused by occurrence in the system situations characterized by the particular states of particular groups of properties, could be described in the form of the system of equations; the same form that is generally used to represents dependence relations of the quantitative systems:

xt+11 = f ( xt1,  xt2,  xt3,  ,   . . .   , xtn );
 xt+12 = f ( xt1,  xt2,  xt3,  ,   . . .   , xtn );
 xt+13 = f ( xt1,  xt2,  xt3,  ,   . . .   , xtn );
 . . . . .
xt+1n = f ( xt1,  xt2,  xt3,  ,   . . .   , xtn );

Despite the noticeable similarity in the general form of description of systems of quantitative and qualitative equations, obvious differences in the way of representation of quantitative and qualitative properties of the modeled systems, however, exist and require the use of different techniques for their modeling.

Quantitative Properties and Modeling Quantitative Systems

We all are well familiar with the systems all properties of which, chosen for modeling, are perceived as numeric (i.e. may be referred to as quantitative) and the four algebraic systems defined on the sets of integer, real or complex numbers. The numbers have a notion of quantities and as the quantities can be added, subtracted, multiplied or divided, four defined on the sets operations, called: addition, subtraction, multiplication and division, allow us to add, subtract, multiply, or divide the numbers. Thus, we have powerful modeling means, making it possible to represent modeled systems’s quantitative properties as a collection of quantitative variables, and to describe the dependence of the values of the variables from the values of other variables in the form of well defined mathematical expressions.

Another way of perceiving the systems and their behavior is to describe them in terms of, as they say, their qualitative estimates or, in the other words, by describing them in the form of statements that serve as the properties’ qualitative assertions and thus lets us referee to the properties as qualitative

Qualitative Properties and Modeling Qualitative Systems

As with modeling of quantitative systems, mathematical modeling of systems all chosen for modeling of which properties are qualitative, - i.e qualitative systems, implies defining a way of representation of the modeled system’s qualitative properties as qualitative aka symbolic variables, specifying the dependency relations of the variables, and describing a machinery providing reproduction, in the model, behavior observed in the modeled system.

However, the properties of symbols that are elements of sets of homogeneous symbols turn out to be quite different from the properties of numbers that are elements of homogeneous sets of numbers.

The main property of numbers used in calculations is the fact that for each pair of numbers belonging to a certain numerical set, each operation defined on the set can always be associated with the third element of the set, which is the result of the operation. This property of numbers and operations with numbers makes it possible to create compositions of operations and thus perform calculations of functions of a rather complex nature.

The properties of symbols and operations with symbols, in comparison with the properties of numbers and operations with numbers, are more limited and the possibilities for creating compositions of operations are less diverse. In contrast to the properties of numbers that allow one to associate a third number with various pairs of numbers, based on the interpretation of the elements of numerical sets as quantities that can be added, subtracted, multiplied and even divided, symbols that are elements of a set of symbols do not have any interpretation and are used as markers to designate various unique state of qualitative variables have only one property corresponding to their application. Each symbol of a set, by definition of the notion of “set,” is a unique element of the set and therefore can only be equivalent to itself and, as a consequence of this, is not equivalent to any other symbol of the set.

The consequence of this characteristic of symbols belonging to a homogeneous set of symbols is the absence of any possibility of direct generation for any two symbolic arguments of a binary operation with symbols of a corresponding resulting symbol of the operation.

So, for instance, binary operations defined on a set consisting of only two symbols solve the constraint problem simply. They simply do not overcome these restrictions. As we know, the total number of binary operations defined on a set of two elements is 16. And none of these operations generates during operation its output element that does not exist in the set. All 16 operations are defined in such a way that no matter what combination of values of the operands of the operation turn out to be, they all always produce the value of one of the operands as their result, thus solving the impossibility of producing any third value.

The resolution to this limitation, as we all well know, however, exists and consists in the inclusion in the process of computation some auxiliary symbols that can be used for representing the intermediate results of comparison of the arguments of the function, which can be used to indicate cases when the compared symbols turn out to be the same, opposite or different.

The implementation of not direct, but, so to speak, indirect transformation of two characters into a third one in the case of following the described approach, of course, implies the implementation of the inverse transformation of the auxiliary symbol into the symbol corresponding to the symbol-values of the arguments. As it was found by experiments on defining operations on an extended set of symbols, carried out in order to confirm the existence of an operation on this set, one argument of which is a constant representing the produced symbol, and the second contains a previously calculated auxiliary symbol and yields the produced symbol in the case when the auxiliary symbol represents fact that the previously transmitted in the comparison operation, two characters were compared or, alternatively, the value of the second argument represented by a symbol indicating that the characters being compared turned out to be different. When continuing to calculate the final value of the function, the auxiliary symbol obtained during its calculation must indicate that the function is partially defined, the auxiliary symbol must be ignored, and the current value of the state of the model variable calculated using the described technique must remain unchanged.

The reasoning about the properties of symbols and the proposed solution given in this section are based on the idea of expanding the domain set of the model by adding to it a set of auxiliary symbols that are the basic solutions that determine the development of techniques for mathematical modeling of qualitative dynamical systems. Development of this approach described here is described further in the sections devoted to determining the requirements for the operations of an algebraic system of operations which are used to determine the functions describing the correspondence of variables of the system model to the situations arising in the system, and in the section describing the Algebraic system determined taking into account the developed requirements for them.

Notion of Qualitative Dynamical System State Space

Whatever types of properties of the modeled systems were considered - quantitative or qualitative, when creating mathematical models of these systems, we proceed from the understanding that all actions and changes in the state of a system itself, or in its external world, that are produced by the organized actions of the system, are always determined by the state in which the system is, at the time of performing the actions.

Therefore, in order to reproduce the behavior of the qualitative systems in the mathematical models, we consider observed dependencies of the state of the properties of systems on the occurrence of certain combinations of the state of other properties, characterizing situations to which the systems react by changing their state. To achieve this, we define a model of a system as a collection of variables representing properties being modeled and the states the properties may take, and associate with each variable a variable state equation v(t+1) = f(v(t)), where the function f computes the value of the variable at time t+1 depending on the state of the system (a combination of the state of other variables of the model of the system) at time t. This makes it possible to reconstruct, with the help of the model, the chain of the changes in the state of the simulated system, observed in its behavior, by repeatedly computing the state equation of the system after each change in the state of the model.

General Computation Technique

While performing various calculations with the quantitative variables, in order to control the course of the calculation process depending on the state of the data, we often have to calculate logical estimates of the truth of various assertions about the state of the data. And then, in the course of the further computation, we check the truth of those assertions in order to direct the process of the computation along the computational branch corresponding to the state of the data. There are, in this process, data on the state of the domain area of the function, from the one hand, and the metadata data state of which characterizes the computational process, on the other hand. These two types of data are not homogeneous. Therefore, when computing, we utilize for calculation only the operations defined on the set of numbers, but never mix them with operations defined on the set of symbols.

Computation of Qualitative Functions

To compute a discrete function means to determine its value for each combination of the states of its arguments. The method of setting such a function for computing discrete dependencies of variables that can take only two values: 0 and 1 is well known. Mechanism of such computation is described in the form of the so-called Disjunctive Normal Form and is implemented as two major steps. First, all expected combinations of state of the system are computed. For that, the conjunction operation, defined on the set {0, 1}, is used. And then, at the second step of the computation, the disjunction operation is applied to combine all the results produced by the conjunction operation. The result of the computation can be value 1, in case when at least one of the results of application of all conjunction operations appeared 1, or the value 0 when none of the situations is recognised. But the modeled properties of the qualitative systems can take not just two, but several states, and this assumes that the state functions of the variables of the qualitative system model must be multivalued. And hence, the operations used to compute the functions must be capable of taking as their arguments and yielding the values of multivalued variables. In other words, they must be defined on the set of symbols, the power of which would allow representing all possible states of the modeled properties.

Along with this, when recognizing situations represented by a set of symbols, we also must compare the current state of variables with their expected state, note the facts of comparison (or non-comparison) of expected values; calculate the conjunctions of the obtained comparison results, check the facts of situation recognition and generate new values of variables corresponding to the recognized situations and change the state of the system in the case when the situation turned out to be present in the model and the fact of its existence made it possible to generate a new state of the variables corresponding to the recognized situation.

As it follows from the described scheme, the calculation procedure does not directly transform the state of its arguments and the value of the function but uses auxiliary variables that provide the calculation of the function but are not the result. Allowing that symbols can be used to represent the results of a comparison creates a unique opportunity to define a set of the algebra as the union of these two sets, and to define the operations required to evaluate a function, which will make it possible to define a function as a composition of these operations. And thus combine the process of calculating the function of the state of the system and the process of conditional calculation of the result of the function into one process that calculates the function as an unconditional process of calculation and in which the calculation control logic is implemented by the operations that perform the transformation of the state of the system.

An analysis of the properties of operations defined on sets of symbols, the number of elements of which is more than two, shows the futility of trying to determine special operations (their number should increase along with the increase in the power of the set), but revealed the possibility of calculating multivalued symbolic functions if a number of auxiliary symbols {} are included in the set, and adding to the function calculation scheme three additional operations named: Equivalence, Production and Application.

To specify a discrete function, in general, means to define a way of computing the values of the function for all possible combinations of states of its arguments. Accordingly, following the existing culture of mathematical thinking, description of mechanism of computation a qualitative function of qualitative arguments should be written as а kind of Extended Disjunctive Normal form enriched with ability to operate with variables capable of taking several different states and return value of the function that may also take several different state.

Representation of Qualitative Dynamical System Model as Matrix State Space Equation

This mathematical modeling technique utilizes an approach based on the notion of vector space of the modeled system. The variety of the states of a modeled qualitative system is characterized by the multitude of combinations of the state of its qualitative properties and therefore can be naturally considered as a qualitative state space of the system. Respectively; the state of the model of the system can be considered as the vector in the state space of the system; the changes in the state of the model can be viewed as the moves of the vector from one point in space to another; and the entire behavior of the model may be seeing as a sequential discret motions of the vector along a dynamically determined broken-line trajectory.

And correspondingly, the modeling technique intended for describing the behavior of the systems, within the framework of this modeling paradigm, presents the models in the form of a vector state equation of the system, which describes the process of changing the state of the system in the form of the algebraic expression consisting of the sequential application of operations of transformation of the current state of the model into its next state. Whereas, the procedure for calculating the equation is supported by an algebraic system defined on the set of symbols, called Algebra of Symbols. The operations of Algebra of Symbols are used in the implementations of the vector operations of transformation of the state of the system.

The general form of such equation can be written as a two-step matrix equation, presented below.

S(t+1) = S(t) < G * R * S(t)      (1)

Or in the form that includes external input, where equation (1) is extended with vector representing external effects on the system at time t.

S(t+1) = S(t) < G * R * (S(t) < I(t))      (2)

Qualitative Dynamical Systems Models Graph Based Representation

Idea of creating a model of dynamical systems was always welcomed. This idea began to take shape even when most computer monitors were alphanumeric, and only a relatively small part of vector monitors allowed to display on their screens some elements connected with lines - mostly monochrome. Now, when huge raster screens of modern monitors make it possible to depict whatever you want, the technique of creating a model of systems in the form of the elements and the links connecting them opens up even more new possibilities. And the Kaleidoscope project did not stay away from this movement either.

Being developed in the Kaleidoscope project, Multicolor Logical Net (McLN), is a graphical modeling formalism intended to be utilized as the qualitative dynamical s ystems modeling means.

Structure of the McLN Model

The graph is constructed of two types of nodes - Property-nodes representing modeled properties and their states, and Condition-nodes representing conditions at which the Property-nodes change their states. Property-nodes are drawn as big circles and their states are depicted as different solid colors that fill the circles. Condition-nodes of the graph are, on the contrary, drawn as small circles. And as the conditions represented by them may be either satisfied or not satisfied, the state of the Condition-nodes is depicted as either white or gray color, where the white color represents the case when condition is satisfied, while the gray color represents the opposite case, i.e. the case when condition is not satisfied. Directed Arcs connect Property-nodes to Condition-nodes.

Arcs directed from Property-nodes to Condition-nodes are Recognizers. Their task is to recognize the expected state of the arc’s input Property-node and transfer the result of recognition to its output Condition-node. In order to be able to accomplish their roles, these arcs are marked with the color known as the expected state of the input Property-nodes of the Arc.

Arcs directed from Condition-nodes to Property-nodes are Generators. Task of these Arcs is to generate the proposed next state of their output Property-nodes. Respectively, these arcs are marked with the color representing the state described in the specification of the response of the modeled system to the situation fact of the recognition of which the Condition-node of the arc is supposed to represent. Color marking the Arc is generated as the proposed state of its output Property-node in case when the state (white color) of the input Condition-node of the Arc indicates that states of all input Property-nodes of this Condition-node are recognized as expected states. Alternatively, that is in the case when the expected situation represented by the input Property-nodes of the Condition-node is not recognized, the Arcs directed from Condition-node to its output Property-nodes send them state (gray color) indicating the fact that the situation expected by the Condition-node is not recognized and thus the output state from this Condition-node should be ignored.

Behavior of the McLN Model

When describing McLN model behavior, we consider all parts of the model: Properties, Conditions and arcs to be simple functional devices. Thus, each part has its input and output and is capable of receiving a signal of a state as its input, processing the signal, and emitting a derived signal as its output. A model is assembled by connecting the outputs of parts to the inputs of other parts. For instance, the outputs of the nodes are connected to the inputs of their output arcs, and the outputs of the arcs are connected to the inputs of their output nodes. The Property nodes' basic function is to take and to hold the property's current state. The arcs from Properties to a Condition are "State Recognizers". The marking state of an arcrecognizer is one of the arc's input Property's possible states that is considered to be the Property Expected State. The role of these arcs is to check if the arc's input Property's state is equal to the arc's Expected State. When Property takes the state that is equal to the arc's expected state, the signal of the fact that the Property is in the expected state is emitted to the arc's Condition. The Condition nodes are their input arcs' output collectors. They take signals from their input arcs and evaluate whether the condition is satisfied. The Condition is considered to be satisfied Share 8/11/23, 1:01 AM McLN Modeling Formalism https://www.dsdsam.com/mcln-mf 4/6 when all its input Properties take the states that are successfully recognized by the Condition's input arcs as the arcs' expected states. The structure composed of a Condition, all its input state recognizing arcs, and all the arcs' input premise Properties forms a fragment that is a rule condition evaluating expression. The combination of all Properties states is considered to be a situation the rule is supposed to recognize. The output of the fragment is the Condition's state that represents the fact that the expected situation is recognized or not recognized. This fact is depicted on the screen as white or gray color respectively. The arcs from a Condition to Properties are "State Generators". They identify the group of Properties the states of which are expected to change when the Condition gets satisfied. These arcs generate the state they are marked with. The states generated by the arcs are offered to the arcs' output Properties to take. The structure composed of a Condition, all its output state generating arcs and all the arc's output Properties form a property state dependency rule that checks the condition and generates the conclusion. This model fragment thus stands for the dependency rule's "If" and "Then" parts. As each Property may have many state-generating input arcs that are connected to different Conditions and hence can produce different offers of state for the Property to take, the "Rule of Concordance" is applied. It sounds as follows: (1) If none of the Property's input Conditions is satisfied, the Property state does not change. (2) If all but one of the Property's input Conditions are not satisfied, the state generated by the Property's input arc connected to the satisfied Condition is taken. (3) If some of the Property's input Conditions are not satisfied while more than one of the other Conditions appear to be satisfied and their output arcs produce the same offer (same state to take), the offer is considered to be accepted and the Property takes the offered state. (4) If some of the Property's input Conditions are not satisfied while more than one of the other Conditions appear to be satisfied and their output arcs produce different offers (different states to take), the offers are considered to be competitive and neutralized. So the Property state does not change. The multiple offered states, thus, are exclusive in the McLN.
